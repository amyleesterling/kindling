How "bang for buck" is scored

A low sticker monthly payment can hide a big upfront check, so a fair comparison has to convert everything to one number. Two standard metrics drive the ranking:

  • Effective Monthly Cost (EMC) = monthly payment + (cash due at signing ÷ lease term). This folds the drive-off back into the payment — the only honest apples-to-apples figure.
  • Lease Ratio = EMC ÷ MSRP, shown as a %. The industry "1% rule": at or below 1.0% is a strong deal; below 0.85% is excellent; above 1.3% is weak.
  • Bang-for-Buck score (BFB) = MSRP ÷ EMC = how many dollars of car you control for every $1 of effective monthly cost. Higher is better. This is the column the table is sorted on.

Money down — and when it's actually cheaper

Putting money down can come out cheaper — but only in specific cases, and the rankings above are already built to see through it. Every score uses Effective Monthly Cost, which folds the drive-off back in, so a low payment propped up by a big down payment doesn't get an unfair edge.

The default case: it's a wash

On a 36-month lease, every $1,000 down lowers the payment by about $28/month ($1,000 ÷ 36), plus a sliver of money-factor savings. So most of the time you're pre-paying, not saving — the total cost barely moves. That's the neutral case.

When money down genuinely wins — real example

Sometimes a manufacturer loads its best lease support into a specific down structure, so the higher-down version costs less in total, not just per month. The Kia Carnival this month is exactly that:

Kia Carnival — same car, two structures$/moDueEff. /mo36-mo total
Less down$445$2,000$501~$18,020
More down ✓ cheaper$379$3,999$490~$17,640

The extra ~$2,000 down drops the payment by $66/mo — and $66 × 36 = $2,376 saved against $1,999 spent, a real ~$377 net saving. The reduction outruns plain amortization, so here, more down is the better deal. But it goes both ways: the Toyota RAV4's Northeast $0-down special (~$399/mo) beats some of its own money-down structures in other regions. The lesson: always get the quote at $0-down and at the advertised money-down, then compare Effective Monthly Cost and pick the lower one.

The real "money down" cheat code: Multiple Security Deposits (MSD)

If you have cash to put down, a down payment is usually the wrong way to use it. MSDs are refundable deposits that lower your money factor (the lease's interest rate) instead of the cap cost — so you cut the monthly and get the money back at lease-end. Toyota, for example, lets you stack up to nine deposits, each shaving the money factor by 0.00008 (≈ a 1.7% rate cut), for a typical $300–$3,000 total saving. BMW, Audi, Lexus, Mercedes, Toyota, Volvo, and Nissan all support MSD programs. Same lower payment as a down payment, but refundable and safe.

The risk of a cash down payment

If the car is totaled or stolen early, your down payment is gone. Gap insurance pays the lender the remaining lease balance — it does not refund your cap-cost reduction. BMW won't cut you a check if it happens in month three. MSDs, by contrast, are refundable even after a total loss. So: don't pile cash down just to hit a monthly target — you're not really saving, and you're taking on loss risk. Use MSDs if the brand offers them; otherwise keep the drive-off low.

Picks for a fast, gas, 4-seat lease

  • Best value + real muscle → Ford Mustang GT. A 480-hp V8, 4 seats, leasing at a 1.10% ratio (~$526/mo effective) — the only performance car that leases like a sensible SUV, because Ford actively subsidizes it. The runaway winner.
  • Cheapest way into a true sports car → Subaru BRZ (~$443/mo effective). Rear-drive, manual, and a 2+2 — four seats, though the rears are token. Currently a better promo than its identical-twin GR86.
  • Speed-per-dollar sweet spot → BMW M240i (382 hp AWD, proper 4 seats) or Subaru WRX (271 hp AWD, roomy 5 seats) for ~$529/mo.
  • Most power with a usable back seat → Dodge Charger (550+ hp, 5 seats) if you want straight-line muscle and don't mind ~$754/mo effective.
  • Worst value → Cadillac CT5-V and Civic Type R — little lease support, so financing is markedly cheaper.

Read on the "3–6 year" term

Leases don't really run 3–6 years. The standard menu is 24, 36, and 39 months. 48-month leases exist but are uncommon; 60- and 72-month "leases" effectively do not exist from manufacturers.

  • 36 months is the value sweet spot — best lease-cash support, and you exit right as the factory warranty (typically 36mo/36k) lapses. Every offer above is normalized to 36 months where available.
  • 48 months lowers the monthly but you keep the car into its costlier, out-of-warranty years and pay more total interest — worse bang-for-buck, not better.
  • If you genuinely want to keep a car 5–6 years, don't lease it — finance/buy it. Leasing is built for 2–4 year cycles; holding past the warranty defeats its purpose.

Takeaways for a Massachusetts shopper

  • Want maximum value? The Mazda CX-70, Nissan Pathfinder, and Hyundai Tucson are the strongest gas SUV picks — all lease at or near the 1% rule.
  • Need a van? The Toyota Sienna LE Hybrid is the clear minivan value leader; Pacifica is close behind.
  • Need a big three-row hauler? The Jeep Grand Wagoneer is the bang-for-buck pick; the Toyota Sequoia Hybrid is the cheapest to run.
  • Want a fast car? The Ford Mustang GT is the only performance car that leases like a sensible vehicle — a 480-hp V8 at a 1.10% ratio. Everything else (BRZ, WRX, M240i, Charger, Type R) leases worse.
  • Always negotiate the drive-off down. Many advertised specials let you pay only "first month + fees." Lowering the cash due at signing improves your real lease ratio directly — and if you want to put cash in, use refundable Multiple Security Deposits instead.

Method, assumptions & caveats

  • Scope is restricted to gasoline and conventional-hybrid vehicles with at least four seats — plug-in battery-electric models and two-seaters are excluded.
  • Payments are advertised June 2026 specials for the Northeast region for well-qualified credit (≈ 700+). Actual MA dealer quotes vary by ZIP, inventory, trim, and credit tier.
  • MSRP figures are approximate, tied to the trim each advertised payment targets, and used only to compute the ratio — verify the exact configured MSRP before signing.
  • Where a payment was published without a due-at-signing figure, the drive-off was standardized to $2,999 (marked *) so every row is comparable; a different real drive-off shifts that row's score.
  • Effective Monthly Cost excludes state/local taxes, title, doc, and acquisition fees, which are similar across vehicles and don't change the ranking materially.
  • Vehicle names link to live lease listings for current pricing; thumbnails are generic studio renders from a public vehicle-image service and may not reflect the exact trim/year.
  • This is decision-support, not a quote. Lease programs reset monthly — re-check before shopping.
